## Problem

An *anti-Pascal triangle* is an equilateral triangular array of numbers such that, except for the numbers in the bottom row, each number is the absolute value of the difference of the two numbers immediately below it. For example, the following is an anti-Pascal triangle with four rows containing every integer from 1 to 10:
$$\begin{array}{ccccccccc} & & & 4 \\ & & 2 & & 6 \\ & 5 & & 7 & & 1 \\ 8 & & 3 & & 10 & & 9 \end{array}$$

Does there exist an anti-Pascal triangle with 2018 rows which contains every integer from 1 to $1 + 2 + \cdots + 2018$?

---

## Solution

**Answer: No.**

Let $n = 2018$ and $N = 1 + 2 + \cdots + n = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$.

**Step 1: The forest structure.**

For every number $d$ not in the bottom row, draw an arrow from $d$ to the *larger* of the two numbers below it (i.e., if $d = a - b$ with $a > b$, draw $d \to a$). This creates an oriented forest (no cycles, since arrows always point to larger numbers).

Consider the directed path $\pi$ starting from the top vertex $A$ (the unique entry of row 1). At each step along $\pi$, we move to a number at least 1 greater. Moreover, the increments along $\pi$ are distinct: if we visit $d_1, d_2, \ldots$ then $d_{i+1} - d_i$ is the value "adjacent" to $d_i$ in the triangle, and all values are distinct.

**Step 2: The path to $N$.**

The sum of the increments along $\pi$ from $A$ to its terminal vertex is at least $1 + 2 + \cdots + (n-1)$, since there are $n-1$ steps and the increments are distinct positive integers. By AM reasoning, $\pi$ terminates at a value of at least $A + 1 + 2 + \cdots + (n-1) = A + N - n$. Since $N$ is the largest value, $\pi$ must terminate at $N$, and all values $\{1, 2, \ldots, n-1\}$ are the increments (appearing as values adjacent to $\pi$).

Let $B$ be the terminal vertex at the bottom row. The path $A \to \cdots \to B$ uses increments $1, 2, \ldots, n-1$ in some order, so the values $1, 2, \ldots, n-1$ all appear adjacent to $\pi$.

**Step 3: The contradiction.**

Let $X$ and $Y$ be the two bottom-row neighbors of $B$ (if $B$ is not at a corner). Consider the second-longest arrow path from some vertex $C$ that is roughly halfway across the top, chosen such that $C$'s path to the bottom hits the bottom at position $D$, which is at horizontal distance at least $\lfloor n/2 \rfloor - 1$ from $B$.

The path from $C$ must use increments that are all $> n - 1$ (since $1, \ldots, n-1$ are occupied near $\pi$). A path of length $\lfloor n/2 \rfloor - 1$ with increments from $\{n, n+1, n+2, \ldots\}$ gives a bottom-row value of at least:
$$n + (n+1) + \cdots + (n + \lfloor n/2 \rfloor - 2) > 1 + 2 + \cdots + n = N$$
for $n \geq 2018$, which is a contradiction since all values lie in $\{1, \ldots, N\}$.

More precisely: take $C$ at the apex of a sub-triangle of side roughly $n/2$. The path from $C$ travels $\lfloor n/2 \rfloor - 1$ steps, so the terminal value is at least $(n+1) + (n+2) + \cdots + (n + \lfloor n/2 \rfloor - 1) > N$. This contradiction shows no such anti-Pascal triangle exists. $\blacksquare$
