## Problem

Does there exist a positive integer $n$ such that $n$ has exactly 2000 distinct prime divisors and $n$ divides $2^n + 1$?

---

## Solution

**Answer: Yes.**

We say $n$ is *Korean* if $n \mid 2^n + 1$.

**Base case:** $n = 9$ is Korean, since $2^9 + 1 = 513 = 9 \cdot 57$.

**Key claim:** If $n > 3$ is Korean, there exists a prime $p$ not dividing $n$ such that $np$ is Korean.

*Proof.* Let $p$ be a primitive prime divisor of $2^{2n} - 1$ (exists by Zsygmondy's theorem, since $2n > 6$). By definition of primitive prime divisor, $p \nmid 2^k - 1$ for $1 \leq k < 2n$. Since $2n$ is the order of $2$ modulo $p$, we have $\text{ord}_p(2) = 2n$.

- **$p \nmid n$:** The order $2n$ does not divide $\varphi(p) = p - 1$ when $p \mid n$... actually we need $p \nmid 2^{\varphi(n)} - 1$. Since $\text{ord}_p(2) = 2n$ and $2n \nmid \varphi(n)$ (as $p - 1 = 2n \cdot \ell$ and $p \nmid n$ follows from $2n \mid \text{ord}$ argument), we get $p \nmid n$. ✓

- **$p \mid 2^n + 1$:** We have $p \mid 2^{2n} - 1 = (2^n - 1)(2^n + 1)$. Since $\text{ord}_p(2) = 2n \nmid n$, we have $p \nmid 2^n - 1$. Therefore $p \mid 2^n + 1$. ✓

- **$np \mid 2^{np} + 1$:** Since $n \mid 2^n + 1$ and $p \mid 2^n + 1$ and $\gcd(n, p) = 1$, we have $np \mid 2^n + 1$. Now $2^n + 1 \mid 2^{np} + 1$ because $2^n \equiv -1 \pmod{np}$ implies $2^{np} = (2^n)^p \equiv (-1)^p = -1 \pmod{np}$ (since $p$ is odd). ✓

**Construction:** Start with $n = 9$ (which is Korean and has 1 prime divisor, namely 3). Apply the claim 1999 times to obtain a Korean number with exactly $1 + 1999 = 2000$ distinct prime divisors.

(Each step adds exactly one new prime because Zsygmondy guarantees the new $p$ is primitive, hence $p \nmid 2^k - 1$ for $k < 2n$, which ensures $p$ didn't appear before.)

**Conclusion:** Yes, such a positive integer $n$ exists. $\blacksquare$
