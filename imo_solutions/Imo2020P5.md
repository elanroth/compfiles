## Problem

A deck of $n > 1$ cards is given. A positive integer is written on each card. The deck has the property that the arithmetic mean of the numbers on each pair of cards is also the geometric mean of the numbers on some collection of one or more cards.

For which $n$ does it follow that the numbers on the cards are all equal?

---

## Solution

**Answer:** For all $n$.

**Setup:** Suppose $a_1, \ldots, a_n$ satisfy the condition but are not all equal. Let $d = \gcd(a_1, \ldots, a_n)$ and replace each $a_i$ by $a_i/d$; the condition is preserved. So assume without loss of generality that $\gcd(a_1, \ldots, a_n) = 1$. Also assume $a_1 \geq a_2 \geq \cdots \geq a_n$.

**Main proof:** Since $a_1 \geq 2$, let $p$ be a prime divisor of $a_1$. Let $k$ be the smallest index such that $p \nmid a_k$; such $k$ exists since $\gcd(a_1, \ldots, a_n) = 1$. In particular $a_1 \neq a_k$.

Consider the arithmetic mean $x = \frac{a_1 + a_k}{2}$. By the condition, $x$ equals the geometric mean of some collection of cards:
$$x = \sqrt[m]{a_{i_1} \cdots a_{i_m}} = \frac{a_1 + a_k}{2} > a_k.$$

Since the arithmetic mean is an integer not divisible by $p$ (as $p \mid a_1$ but $p \nmid a_k$, so $a_1 + a_k \not\equiv 0 \pmod{2p}$... actually $x = (a_1 + a_k)/2$ where $p \mid a_1$ and $p \nmid a_k$, so $p \nmid (a_1 + a_k)$, hence $p \nmid x$), all indices $i_1, \ldots, i_m$ must satisfy $p \nmid a_{i_j}$ — otherwise $p \mid \text{GM}^m$ giving $p \mid \text{GM}$ — so $p \mid x$, contradiction.

But all cards with $p \nmid a_{i_j}$ satisfy $a_{i_j} \leq a_k$ (by choice of $k$ as the smallest index with $p \nmid a_k$, and we ordered $a_1 \geq \cdots \geq a_n$). Therefore the geometric mean satisfies:
$$x = \sqrt[m]{a_{i_1} \cdots a_{i_m}} \leq a_k,$$
contradicting $x > a_k$.

This contradiction shows the cards cannot fail to be all equal. $\blacksquare$

*Remark: Since $\frac{27+9}{2} = 18 = \sqrt[3]{27 \cdot 27 \cdot 8}$, the hypothesis really is necessary: the AM of two largest different cards CAN equal the GM of other cards. The key is the global argument about the largest card.*
