## Problem

Let $k$ be a positive integer and let $S$ be a finite set of odd prime numbers. Prove that there is at most one way (up to rotation and reflection) to place the elements of $S$ around the circle such that the product of any two neighbors is of the form $x^2 + x + k$ for some positive integer $x$.

---

## Solution

We replace "positive integer $x$" with "nonneg integer $x$", and say $n$ is *good* if $n = x^2 + x + k$ for some $x \geq 0$. (Equivalently for integer $x$, since $x \mapsto -1-x$ gives the same value.)

**Key Claim 1:** If $p$ is an odd prime, there are at most **two** odd primes $q < p$ for which $pq$ is good. Moreover, if $pq = x^2 + x + k$ and $pr = y^2 + y + k$ with $x, y \geq 0$, then $x + y + 1 = p$ and $xy \equiv k \pmod p$.

*Proof.* The equation $T^2 + T + k \equiv 0 \pmod p$ has at most 2 solutions modulo $p$. Since $0 \leq x, y < p$ (as $p > q, r$ and $k > 0$), there are at most two such pairs. By Vieta's formulas applied to the quadratic mod $p$: $x + y \equiv -1 \pmod p$ (so $x + y + 1 = p$ since $0 < x + y < 2p$) and $xy \equiv k \pmod p$. $\square$

**Key Claim 2:** If both $pq$ and $pr$ are good (with $p$ the largest), then $qr$ is also good.

*Proof.* Let $\alpha \in \mathbb{C}$ with $\alpha^2 + \alpha + k = 0$. For any integer $n$, $n^2 + n + k = \text{Norm}(n - \alpha)$ where Norm is from $\mathbb{Q}(\alpha)/\mathbb{Q}$.

Then:
$$pq \cdot pr = \text{Norm}((x-\alpha)(y-\alpha)) = \text{Norm}((xy - k) - (x+y+1)\alpha).$$

By Claim 1, $x + y + 1 = p$ and $xy - k \equiv 0 \pmod p$. Write $xy - k = pc'$ and $x+y+1 = p$. So:
$$pq \cdot pr = \text{Norm}(pc' - p\alpha) = p^2 \cdot \text{Norm}(c' - \alpha).$$

Hence $qr = \text{Norm}(c' - \alpha) = (c')^2 + c' + k$, which is good (with $x = c'$, verified to be a nonneg integer). $\square$

**Proof of uniqueness:** We proceed by induction on $|S|$. Given a circular arrangement, consider the largest prime $p \in S$. Its two neighbors $q$ and $r$ satisfy $pq$ and $pr$ both good, so by Claim 1 there are at most two choices for $\{q, r\}$. By Claim 2, $qr$ is also good, so $q$ and $r$ are adjacent in any arrangement of $S \setminus \{p\}$.

By induction, $S \setminus \{p\}$ has at most one circular arrangement (up to rotation/reflection). And $p$ can only be inserted between $q$ and $r$. So there is at most one arrangement of $S$. $\blacksquare$

*Remark: The author constructed a ring of 385 primes for $k = 41$, showing the condition is not vacuous.*
