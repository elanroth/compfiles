## Problem

Let $ABC$ be an equilateral triangle. Let $A_1, B_1, C_1$ be interior points of $ABC$ such that $BA_1 = A_1C$, $CB_1 = B_1A$, $AC_1 = C_1B$, and
$$\angle BA_1C + \angle CB_1A + \angle AC_1B = 480°.$$
Let $A_2 = BC_1 \cap CB_1$, $B_2 = CA_1 \cap AC_1$, $C_2 = AB_1 \cap BA_1$. Prove that if triangle $A_1B_1C_1$ is scalene, then the circumcircles of triangles $AA_1A_2$, $BB_1B_2$, and $CC_1C_2$ all pass through two common points.

---

## Solution

*(Second official solution from the marking scheme.)*

Set $\alpha := \angle A_1CB = \angle CBA_1$, $\beta := \angle ACB_1 = \angle B_1AC$, $\gamma := \angle C_1AB = \angle C_1BA$, so $\alpha + \beta + \gamma = 30°$.

Since $\max(\alpha,\beta,\gamma) < 30°$, each of $A_1, B_1, C_1$ lies strictly inside the triangle (e.g., $A_1$ inside $\triangle OBC$ where $O$ is the center). The hexagon $A_2C_1B_2A_1C_2B_1$ is convex.

**Claim 1:** $A_1$ is the circumcenter of $\triangle A_2BC$.

*Proof.* $\angle BA_1C = 180° - 2\alpha$. Also $\angle BA_2C = 180° - \angle CBC_1 - \angle B_1CB = 180° - (60°-\gamma) - (60°-\beta) = 60°+\beta+\gamma = 90° - \alpha = \frac{1}{2}\angle BA_1C$. Since $A_1$ lies inside $\triangle BA_2C$, it is the circumcenter. $\square$

**Claim 2:** Quadrilateral $B_2C_1B_1C$ is cyclic (call its circumcircle $\gamma_a$). Similarly define $\gamma_b$ and $\gamma_c$. The three circles are pairwise distinct.

*Proof.* Using directed angles: $\measuredangle B_2B_1C_2 = 60° + 2\alpha$ and $\measuredangle B_2C_1C_2 = 60° + 2\alpha$, so $B_2C_1B_1C$ is cyclic.

For distinctness: if the hexagon were cyclic, then $\angle BA_1C + \angle CB_1A + \angle AC_1B = 360° \neq 480°$, contradiction. $\square$

**Main argument using coaxiality:**

Let line $AA_1$ meet $\gamma_b$ and $\gamma_c$ again at $X$ and $Y$. Define $k_a := AX/AY$. By the coaxiality lemma, the locus
$$\mathcal{C}_a := \{P : \text{Pow}(P, \gamma_b) = k_a \cdot \text{Pow}(P, \gamma_c)\}$$
is a circle (since $k_a \neq 1$ when $A_1B_1C_1$ is scalene). Since $A_1$, $A_2$, and $A$ all lie on $\mathcal{C}_a$ (their powers satisfy the ratio), $\mathcal{C}_a$ is the circumcircle of $\triangle AA_1A_2$.

Similarly, $\mathcal{C}_b$ is the circumcircle of $\triangle BB_1B_2$ with $k_b := BY/BX'$, and $\mathcal{C}_c$ is the circumcircle of $\triangle CC_1C_2$ with $k_c$.

**Key relation:** A computation using the law of sines gives:
$$k_a = \frac{AB_1}{AC_1} \cdot \frac{\sin(30°+\beta)}{\sin(30°+\gamma)}.$$

By the cyclic symmetry of the construction, $k_a k_b k_c = 1$.

**Conclusion:** Since $k_a k_b k_c = 1$, the three "coaxiality equations" are:
$$\mathcal{C}_a: \text{Pow}(\cdot, \gamma_b) = k_a \text{Pow}(\cdot,\gamma_c), \quad \mathcal{C}_b: \text{Pow}(\cdot,\gamma_c) = k_b \text{Pow}(\cdot, \gamma_a), \quad \mathcal{C}_c: \text{Pow}(\cdot, \gamma_a) = k_c \text{Pow}(\cdot,\gamma_b).$$

Any point on two of these circles satisfies the third (since $k_a k_b k_c = 1$). The convexity of the hexagon ensures the three circles pairwise intersect in two distinct points, and since any intersection of two circles lies on the third, all three share the same two common points. $\blacksquare$
