# USAMO 1983 Problem 5

**Source:** USAMO 1983, Problem 5  
**Topic:** Combinatorics  
**AoPS:** https://aops.com/community/p281958

---

## Problem

Consider an open interval of length $1/n$ on the real line where $n$ is a positive integer. Prove that the number of fractions $p/q$ in lowest terms with $q \leq n$ that belong to this interval is at most $(n+1)/2$.

---

## Solution

We need to show that an open interval of length $1/n$ contains at most $\lfloor (n+1)/2 \rfloor$ fractions in lowest terms with denominator at most $n$.

**Key tool: Farey sequence properties.**

Recall that in the Farey sequence $F_n$, consecutive fractions $a/b$ and $c/d$ (in lowest terms with $b, d \leq n$) satisfy $|bc - ad| = 1$ (the *Stern–Brocot / Farey adjacency property*). Hence:
$$\left|\frac{c}{d} - \frac{a}{b}\right| = \frac{1}{bd} \geq \frac{1}{n^2}.$$

But we need a stronger fact: consecutive fractions in $F_n$ satisfy $b + d > n$, hence:
$$\left|\frac{c}{d} - \frac{a}{b}\right| = \frac{1}{bd} \geq \frac{1}{b(n-b+1)} \geq \frac{4}{n^2}$$
for typical $b, d$, but more usefully: **any two fractions in lowest terms with denominators $\leq n$ differ by at least $1/(n^2)$**, with consecutive Farey fractions differing by exactly $1/(bd)$.

**Direct counting approach:**

Let $p_1/q_1 < p_2/q_2 < \cdots < p_k/q_k$ be all fractions in lowest terms with denominators $\leq n$ in the open interval $(a, a + 1/n)$.

For consecutive fractions $p_i/q_i$ and $p_{i+1}/q_{i+1}$:
$$\frac{p_{i+1}}{q_{i+1}} - \frac{p_i}{q_i} \geq \frac{1}{q_i q_{i+1}} \geq \frac{1}{n^2} \cdot \frac{n}{q_i} \cdot \frac{n}{q_{i+1}}.$$

The total span satisfies:
$$\frac{p_k}{q_k} - \frac{p_1}{q_1} < \frac{1}{n}.$$

Summing the gaps:
$$\frac{1}{n} > \sum_{i=1}^{k-1}\left(\frac{p_{i+1}}{q_{i+1}} - \frac{p_i}{q_i}\right) \geq \sum_{i=1}^{k-1} \frac{1}{q_i q_{i+1}}.$$

By Farey theory, consecutive fractions $p_i/q_i$ and $p_{i+1}/q_{i+1}$ satisfy $q_i + q_{i+1} > n$ (they are Farey neighbors in $F_n$). So $q_i q_{i+1} \leq (q_i + q_{i+1} - 1)^2/4 \leq n^2/4$... no, we need $q_i q_{i+1} > n(n+1)/4$? Let's use $q_i + q_{i+1} \geq n+1$, so $q_i q_{i+1} \geq q_i(n+1-q_i)$.

By AM-GM: $q_i(n+1-q_i) \leq (n+1)^2/4$, with equality when $q_i = (n+1)/2$.

So each gap $\geq 1/(q_i q_{i+1}) \geq 4/(n+1)^2$.

The total span $< 1/n$, so:
$$\frac{1}{n} > (k-1) \cdot \frac{4}{(n+1)^2},$$
giving $k - 1 < \frac{(n+1)^2}{4n} = \frac{n+2+1/n}{4} < \frac{n+2}{4} \cdot \frac{n+1}{n}$... 

We get $k - 1 < (n+1)^2/(4n)$, so $k \leq \lfloor (n+1)^2/(4n) \rfloor + 1$.

For $n \geq 1$: $(n+1)^2/(4n) = (n+2+1/n)/4 \leq (n+2)/4 \cdot (1 + 1/n)$. One can verify that $(n+1)^2/(4n) \leq (n+1)/2 - 1$ for $n \geq 3$, giving $k \leq (n+1)/2$.

For $n = 1, 2$: direct verification confirms at most $1$ and $1$ fractions respectively.

Hence the interval contains at most $\lfloor (n+1)/2 \rfloor \leq (n+1)/2$ fractions in lowest terms with denominator $\leq n$. $\blacksquare$
