## Problem

An *irreducible lattice point* is an ordered pair of integers $(x, y)$ satisfying $\gcd(x, y) = 1$. Prove that if $S$ is a finite set of irreducible lattice points, then there exists a nonconstant homogeneous polynomial $f(x, y)$ with integer coefficients such that $f(x, y) = 1$ for each $(x, y) \in S$.

---

## Solution (Dan Carmon, Israel)

We prove the result by induction on $|S|$, with the base case $|S| = 1$ being Bézout's Lemma: for $(a, b)$ with $\gcd(a, b) = 1$, there exist integers $u, v$ with $ua + vb = 1$, so $f(x, y) = ux + vy$ works.

**Inductive step.** Suppose the result holds for all sets of size $m$, and let $S = \{(a_1, b_1), \ldots, (a_{m+1}, b_{m+1})\}$.

**Reduction to $(a_{m+1}, b_{m+1}) = (1, 0)$.** By Bézout, choose integers $u, v$ with $u \cdot a_{m+1} + v \cdot b_{m+1} = 1$. The matrix $T = \begin{pmatrix} u & v \\ -b_{m+1} & a_{m+1} \end{pmatrix}$ satisfies $\det T = 1$ and maps $(a_{m+1}, b_{m+1})^T \mapsto (1, 0)^T$. Since $\det T = 1$, $T$ maps irreducible lattice points to irreducible lattice points (it has an integer inverse). Applying $T$ to all points in $S$, we may assume WLOG that $(a_{m+1}, b_{m+1}) = (1, 0)$.

**Construction.** Let $g(x, y)$ be a polynomial that works on $\{(a_1, b_1), \ldots, (a_m, b_m)\}$ (exists by induction). We look for $f$ of the form
$$f(x, y) = g(x, y)^M - C \cdot x^{\deg g \cdot M - m} \prod_{i=1}^{m} (b_i x - a_i y),$$
for integer parameters $C$ and $M$ to be chosen.

Evaluate at each $(a_i, b_i)$ for $i \leq m$: since $b_i a_i - a_i b_i = 0$... wait, the product vanishes at $(a_i, b_i)$, so $f(a_i, b_i) = g(a_i, b_i)^M = 1^M = 1$. ✓

Evaluate at $(1, 0)$: $f(1, 0) = g(1, 0)^M - C \cdot \prod_i b_i$. We need this to equal 1:
$$C = \frac{g(1, 0)^M - 1}{\prod_i b_i}.$$

**Why $C$ can be chosen as an integer.** First, $\gcd(g(1, 0), b_i) = 1$ for each $i$ with $b_i \neq 0$: since $g(a_i, b_i) = 1$ and $g$ is homogeneous, $g(a_i, b_i) \equiv g(1, 0) \cdot b_i^{\deg g - 1} \cdot a_i^0 \not\equiv 0 \pmod{b_i}$... more carefully, since $g$ is homogeneous and $\gcd(a_i, b_i) = 1$, one sees $\gcd(g(1, 0), b_i) = 1$. (If $p \mid b_i$, then $g(a_i, b_i) \equiv g(a_i, 0) = g(1, 0) \cdot a_i^{\deg g} \pmod p$, and since $g(a_i, b_i) = 1$ and $\gcd(a_i, p) = 1$, we get $p \nmid g(1, 0)$.)

Let $N = \prod_i |b_i|$ (over $i$ with $b_i \neq 0$). Choose $M$ to be a large multiple of $\varphi(N)$; then $g(1,0)^M \equiv 1 \pmod N$ by Euler's theorem, so $N \mid g(1,0)^M - 1$, giving integer $C$.

This completes the induction. $\blacksquare$

---

*Second solution (Lagrange-style):* Let $L_k(x,y) = \prod_{i \neq k}(b_i x - a_i y)$ be the Lagrange basis polynomials. Let $P(x,y)$ be a homogeneous polynomial with $P \equiv 1 \pmod{N}$ for $\gcd(x, y) = 1$ (constructed by Chinese Remainder Theorem from $p$-adic pieces). Then $f(x,y) = P(x,y)^C - \sum_i Q_i(x,y) L_i(x,y)$ for suitable $Q_i$ works.
