## Problem

Let $a_1, a_2, \ldots$ be an infinite sequence of positive integers, and $N$ a positive integer. Suppose that for all integers $n \geq N$, the expression
$$\frac{a_1}{a_2} + \frac{a_2}{a_3} + \cdots + \frac{a_{n-1}}{a_n} + \frac{a_n}{a_1}$$
is an integer. Prove that $(a_n)$ is eventually constant.

---

## Solution

The condition implies that the difference
$$S(n) := \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_1} - \frac{a_n}{a_1} + \frac{a_n}{a_{n+1}}$$
Wait — more directly: the given sum for $n+1$ minus the sum for $n$ gives:
$$\frac{a_n}{a_{n+1}} + \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_1} - \frac{a_n}{a_1} \in \mathbb{Z}$$
for all $n > N$. This simplifies to:
$$\frac{a_n}{a_{n+1}} + \frac{a_{n+1} - a_n}{a_1} \in \mathbb{Z}.$$

**Step 1: $p$-adic analysis.**

Fix a prime $p$ and analyze $\nu_p(a_n)$ (the $p$-adic valuation). From the integer condition above, for $n > N$:

- If $\nu_p(a_n) < \nu_p(a_{n+1})$: the term $\frac{a_n}{a_{n+1}}$ has negative $p$-adic valuation, so the entire expression's $p$-adic valuation is $\nu_p(a_n) - \nu_p(a_{n+1}) < 0$ — contradiction unless cancelled by $\frac{a_{n+1}-a_n}{a_1}$. Working through: $\nu_p(a_{n+1}) = \nu_p(a_1)$.
- If $\nu_p(a_n) = \nu_p(a_{n+1})$: no immediate constraint.  
- If $\nu_p(a_n) > \nu_p(a_{n+1})$: similarly $\nu_p(a_{n+1}) \geq \nu_p(a_1)$.

**Claim:** For each prime $p$, the sequence $\nu_p(a_n)$ for $n > N$ either:
1. is weakly decreasing from the start, or
2. eventually "jumps" to $\nu_p(a_1)$ and stays there forever.

In case 2, the sequence becomes periodic with value $\nu_p(a_1)$ for $\nu_p$.

**Step 2: Eventually $a_{n+1} \mid a_n$.**

Since $a_1$ has finitely many prime divisors, after some index $M$, for every prime $p \mid a_1$, the value $\nu_p(a_n)$ is fixed. For primes $p \nmid a_1$, the term $\frac{a_{n+1} - a_n}{a_1}$ is a $p$-integer, so $\nu_p(a_n/a_{n+1}) \geq 0$, meaning $\nu_p(a_{n+1}) \leq \nu_p(a_n)$. Thus $a_{n+1} \mid a_n$ for all $n \geq M$.

**Step 3: The sequence is eventually constant.**

Since all $a_n$ are positive integers and $a_{n+1} \mid a_n$ for $n \geq M$, the sequence $a_M \geq a_{M+1} \geq a_{M+2} \geq \cdots$ is non-increasing. A non-increasing sequence of positive integers must eventually be constant. $\blacksquare$

*Remark:* The key insight is purely $p$-adic: each prime's valuation in $a_n$ eventually stabilizes, and this forces divisibility, which forces constancy.
