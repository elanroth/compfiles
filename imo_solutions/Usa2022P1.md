## Problem

Let $a$ and $b$ be positive integers. Every cell of an $(a+b+1) \times (a+b+1)$ grid is colored either amber or bronze such that there are at least $a^2 + ab - b$ amber cells and at least $b^2 + ab - a$ bronze cells. Prove that it is possible to choose $a$ amber cells and $b$ bronze cells such that no two of the $a+b$ chosen cells lie in the same row or column.

---

## Solution

**Step 1: Find transversals $T_a$ (mostly amber) and $T_b$ (mostly bronze).**

**Claim:** There exists a transversal $T_a$ with at least $a$ amber cells; there exists a transversal $T_b$ with at least $b$ bronze cells.

*Proof.* If we pick a uniformly random transversal (one cell per row and column), the expected number of amber cells is at least $\frac{a^2+ab-b}{(a+b+1)^2} \cdot (a+b+1) = \frac{a^2+ab-b}{a+b+1} = a - 1 + \frac{1}{a+b+1} > a-1$.

Since the expected value exceeds $a-1$, there exists a transversal with $\geq a$ amber cells.

By a symmetric argument (using $b^2+ab-a \geq b^2+ab-a$), there exists a transversal $T_b$ with $\geq b$ bronze cells.

**Step 2: Interpolate between $T_a$ and $T_b$.**

Transform $T_a$ into $T_b$ by a sequence of *swaps*: choose two cells $c$ and $c'$ not in the same row or column, and swap them with the two other corners of the rectangle they determine:

$$\begin{pmatrix} c & \cdot \\ \cdot & c' \end{pmatrix} \longrightarrow \begin{pmatrix} \cdot & c \\ c' & \cdot \end{pmatrix}$$

Each such swap keeps the transversal property (one cell per row and column) and changes the number of amber cells by $-2$, $-1$, $0$, $+1$, or $+2$ depending on the colors involved.

**Claim (Discrete IVT):** During the transformation from $T_a$ (with $\geq a$ amber cells) to $T_b$ (with $\geq b$ bronze cells, i.e., $\leq a+1$ amber cells), at some intermediate transversal the number of amber cells is exactly $a$ or $a+1$.

*Proof.* Each swap changes the amber count by at most 1 in absolute value (after choosing the swap carefully). So by the discrete intermediate value theorem, we pass through a transversal with exactly $a$ amber cells. $\square$

**Step 3: Extract the desired cells.**

At the transversal with exactly $a$ amber cells (and $b+1$ non-amber = bronze cells since $a + b + 1$ total), choose the $a$ amber cells and $b$ of the $b+1$ bronze cells. These $a+b$ cells lie in distinct rows and columns (since they are a subset of a transversal). $\blacksquare$
