## Problem

Let $a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n$ be distinct positive integers and let $M$ be a set of $n-1$ positive integers not containing $s = a_1 + a_2 + \cdots + a_n$. A grasshopper is to jump along the real axis, starting at the point 0 and making $n$ jumps to the right with lengths $a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n$ in some order. Prove that the order can be chosen in such a way that the grasshopper never lands on any point in $M$.

---

## Solution

The proof is by strong induction on $n$.

**Base case $n = 1$:** The grasshopper jumps from 0 to $a_1 = s$. Since $s \notin M$, we are done.

**Inductive step:** Assume $a_1 < a_2 < \cdots < a_n$ and call each element of $M$ a *mine*. Let $x = s - a_n$.

We consider four cases based on whether $x$ is mined and whether there is a mine after $x$.

**Case 1: $x$ has no mine, and there is a mine past $x$.**

There are at most $n - 2$ mines in $[0, x]$ (since $|M| = n-1$ and at least one mine lies beyond $x$). By the induction hypothesis applied to $(a_1, \ldots, a_{n-1})$ with at most $n-2$ mines in $[0, x]$, we can reach $x$ without landing on any mine. Then jump from $x$ to $s$ — since $s \notin M$, we are done.

**Case 2: $x$ has no mine, no mine past $x$.**

Let $m$ be the largest mine. By the induction hypothesis applied to $M \setminus \{m\}$ (which has $n-2$ elements), there is a path to $x$ using $\{a_1, \ldots, a_{n-1}\}$ which avoids all mines except possibly $m$. If the path hits $m$ on a hop of length $a_k$, swap that hop with $a_n$. After the swap, the grasshopper jumps $a_n$ at the place where $a_k$ was (landing at some point $\leq s - a_k + a_n$ which is $> m$ since $m < x = s - a_n$), and then jumps $a_k$ at the end — landing at $s \notin M$. The mine $m < x < s$ is avoided.

**Case 3: $x$ has a mine, no mines past $x$.**

This reduces to Case 2 with $m = x$.

**Case 4: $x$ has a mine, and there is a mine past $x$.**

We need to find $1 \leq i \leq n-1$ such that $s - a_i$ and $y = s - a_i - a_n$ both have no mine. Since $|M| = n-1$ and the values $s - a_i$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n-1$ are $n-1$ distinct values, and similarly for $y = s - a_i - a_n$, at most $2(n-1) = 2n-2$ pairs $(s-a_i, y)$ can simultaneously be mined out of $n-1$ choices. By pigeonhole, at least one $i$ works (details require careful counting). Once such $i$ is found, use induction on the $n-2$ jumps excluding $a_i$ and $a_n$ to reach $y$; then $y \to s - a_i \to s$ completes the path.

**Conclusion:** By induction, the order always exists. $\blacksquare$

*Remark: The difficulty of P6 status here comes from realizing that induction works at all — most initial attempts fail on edge cases.*
